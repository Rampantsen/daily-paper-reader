# Geometric planted matchings in high dimensions: The power of multiple views 

Timothy L. H. Wee вИЧ Kaylee Y. Yang вА† Zhou Fan вА° Cheng Mao ¬І

Abstract 

We study the problem of recovering the correspondence between a collection of n points in 

Rd and a noisy, permuted version of those points. In the high-dimensional regime d = ѕЙ(log n), under a Gaussian model with noise variance ѕГ2 = d/ (b log n), prior work identifies b = 2 as the threshold for almost exact recovery. We prove that this threshold is all-or-nothing: for every fixed b < 2, no estimator recovers a positive fraction of the matching, and even estimating the matched point cloud in Euclidean distance is asymptotically no better than ignoring the correspondence. On the other hand, we consider a multi-view generalization of the problem where K noisy, independently permuted copies of the same latent point cloud are observed. Here we show that a simple polynomial-time procedure recovers all relative matchings up to 

o(n) errors whenever b > K/ (K вИТ 1). Thus multiple views can break the impossibility barrier 

b = 2 for the original matching problem: in particular, for 3 /2 < b < 2, the two-view model has no nontrivial recovery, but a third view makes all latent correspondences efficiently recoverable.

# 1 Introduction 

Let X1, . . . , X n be a point cloud consisting of independent standard Gaussian vectors in Rd. In the geometric planted matching model, one observes the original point cloud as well as a noisy permuted copy 

Yi = Xќ†вИЧ(i) + ѕГZ i, i = 1 , . . . , n, (1) where the noise Z1, . . . , Z n are independent standard Gaussian vectors in Rd, ѕГ is the noise scaling, and ќ† вИЧ is an unknown uniformly random permutation, viewed as a bijection on [ n]. The goal is to recover ќ† вИЧ.This model captures the fundamental task of identifying unknown correspondences between un-ordered objects from noisy features. Such matching problems arise across science and engineering, including in single-cell data integration and multi-omics alignment [HLMM18, DSS +22, CJM +22], particle tracking [CKK +10], image matching [MJF +21], record linkage [SBSBS16], and network alignment [BGG +09]. Across these settings, the same statistical and algorithmic question recurs: what level of noise can the observations tolerate so that the latent correspondence remains recov-erable, and when can this be done efficiently? Our results and techniques pertain to the high-dimensional regime where d = ѕЙ(log n) and where the noise scales as 

ѕГ2 = db log n ,

where b > 0 is a fixed parameter. This is the critical scaling in which sharp recovery transitions occur at constant values of b.Much of the existing theory for geometric planted matching has focused on exact or almost exact recovery , where one must identify all but o(n) of the matched pairs. Prior results [KNW22, DCK23, WWXY22] place the almost exact recovery threshold at b = 2. However, this still leaves open the nature of the recovery landscape below the threshold for this canonical model. In particular, for 

b < 2, it is plausible that an estimator might still recover a positive fraction of the matching, or might localize the matched point cloud well in Euclidean distance, producing a вАЬsomethingвАЭ phase that straddles between almost exact recovery and total failure. In this paper we resolve this uncertainty. Our results rule out the presence of a вАЬsomethingвАЭ phase for b < 2. Instead we show that the model is in a strong вАЬnothingвАЭ phase: the posterior assigns exponentially small mass to the set of permutations that agree with the truth on any fixed positive fraction of indices. In particular, no estimator can achieve nonvanishing overlap with the true permutation ќ† вИЧ. Furthermore, this failure is not only combinatorial: even if one only asks to reconstruct the matched point cloud ( Xќ†вИЧ(1) , . . . , X ќ†вИЧ(n)) in Euclidean distance, the observations are asymptotically no more informative than in the case where the correspondence is completely absent. Thus, for b < 2, the data support neither weak recovery of the permutation nor geometrically accurate matching of the points. On the other hand, multiple views reveal a different side of the same matching problem. In-formally, instead of one noisy copy, we observe K independently relabeled noisy views of the same point cloud X1, . . . , X n,

The effect of multiple views is not obvious a priori. Additional observations of the same latent point cloud are obtained, but more latent matchings have to be estimated. At the same time, multiple views create consistency checks that are absent from the two-view problem: a proposed tuple of matches must agree across many pairwise comparisons. Already for K = 3, this gives a clean illustration of the gain from multiple views: for 3 /2 < b < 2, two views give us nothing, but an additional third view breaks this barrier and permits efficient recovery of all 

relative matchings. In addition, by data processing, our negative results transfers over to geometric graph matching models [WWXY22] that are stochastically degraded variants of (1). 

1.1 Related work 

Planted matchings. The overarching goal in these problems is to uncover a hidden correspon-dence between two collections of objects based on noisy observations. A mathematical formulation arose in the context of particle tracking [CKK +10], where geometric observations determine the like-lihood of pairing particles across images. A parallel, non-geometric line studied independent-edge planted matching models [SSZ20, MMX21, DWXY23, WM25, ABAL +26]. Closer to the geometric motivation, [KNW22, DCK23] studied Gaussian point-cloud matching, with related work going beyond Gaussianity [dRSO24], models with low-rank signals [CJM +22], and uncertainty quantifi-cation in match probabilities [FWY26]. A multiple view setting in the context of non-geometric Gaussian graph alignment was studied in [EG25]. 

High-dimensional geometric matching. The geometric matching model (1) in the high-dimensional setting of d = ѕЙ(log n) and ѕГ2 = d/ (b log n) has been studied by [KNW22, DCK23, WWXY22]. A major focus in [KNW22, DCK23] is the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE), which corresponds to the solution of a linear assignment problem with weights induced by the two observed point clouds. For b > 4, Kunisky and Niles-Weed [KNW22] proves that the MLE achieves exact recovery. For 

b < 4, they instead conjectured that the MLE makes ќ©( n) errors. Additionally, a heuristic analysis of a row-wise inner-product procedure is provided, predicting almost exact recovery for b > 2. Dai et al. [DCK23] studied an asymptotically equivalent setting from the perspective of database alignment. Their results determine polynomial error rates for the MLE throughout the intermediate regime 2 < b < 4. In particular, [DCK23] asserts that the MLE attains almost exact recovery for every b > 2. This contrasts with the high-dimensional conjectural picture in [KNW22], while agreeing with the proved exact recovery threshold at b = 4. Wang et al. [WWXY22] studied geometric graph observations generated from latent point clouds (1). In order to prove negative results, [WWXY22] gave a necessary condition ruling out almost exact recovery for b < 2 in the more stochastically informative model (1), agreeing with and 3supplementing the results of [KNW22, DCK23]. Our results strengthen this by ruling out the possibility of nontrivial recovery for b < 2. Furthermore, our results also rule out a вАЬsomethingвАЭ phase for the geometric graph models of [WWXY22] below the critical threshold. Our proofs are based on a conditional second moment method which differs from the rate distortion approach in [WWXY22].
